Designing Systems That Scale

The best interfaces feel effortless — yet behind that simplicity lies a carefully considered system of constraints. Here's how a deliberate typography foundation shapes every screen.

Start With a Scale, Not a Guess

Most design inconsistencies trace back to the same mistake: picking font sizes one at a time, by feel. Instead, define a proportional scale up front — a fixed ratio applied recursively from a base size. The Major Third (1.25) strikes the right balance between visual variety and coherence, giving you seven distinct sizes without the jarring jumps of the Golden Ratio.

Once the scale is set, resist the temptation to introduce arbitrary sizes. Every deviation forces viewers to parse whether the difference is meaningful. Constraints that look restrictive on paper feel liberating in practice — decisions become faster, and the result looks intentional.

Weight and Tracking Work Together

Size alone doesn't create hierarchy — weight and letter spacing complete the picture. Large display text benefits from negative tracking that pulls glyphs together, compensating for the optical gap that opens up at high point sizes. Small all-caps labels need wide tracking to remain legible, not narrow. Treat each axis as a lever you can push in either direction.

A good rule of thumb: increase weight going up the hierarchy, tighten tracking as size grows, and loosen it as size shrinks. This keeps the visual texture consistent even when individual values differ.

Colour as a Supporting Role

Reach for colour contrast rather than additional weights when you need to de-emphasise text. A secondary ink at 55% opacity reads as subordinate without adding visual noise. Reserve your accent colour for interactive labels, badges, and inline links — not for decoration.
